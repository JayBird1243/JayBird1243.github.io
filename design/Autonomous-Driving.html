Autonomous Driving

← Back to Design

Overview

A semester-long team project to build a fully autonomous robot capable of navigating a miniature city, accepting passenger fares, and obeying traffic rules — all in real time on the constrained hardware of a Raspberry Pi. The robot combined graph-based path planning, computer vision lane tracking, hardware-accelerated object detection, and a live fare-dispatch server to simulate a scaled-down autonomous taxi.

Most individual components reached a functional baseline. Pathfinding and fare logic ran correctly, lane following held for ~7 seconds on straight paths, and object detection identified signs at 0.67+ confidence in controlled testing. The competition was cancelled before full integration could be completed, but the architecture was sound — remaining gaps were tuning problems, not design failures.

My Contributions

What I Built

My work centred on the systems that determined where the vehicle went and why — the intelligence layer above raw motion. I also built the vehicle's brake and turn lights from scratch.

Primary · Navigation & Routing

Pathfinding & Position Tracking

Designed the full route intelligence layer: modified Dijkstra's algorithm with custom edge weights, plus a dead-reckoning position fallback for when GPS was too slow.

Implemented via NetworkX with edges weighted by distance plus a priority value — risk zone roads were penalised to appear longer, and any branch where the vehicle changed direction had its effective distance doubled, naturally discouraging high-turn routes. The competition's VPFS GPS had up to 4-second latency, so I built a dead-reckoning layer that polled vehicle speed and heading to keep an internal x/y position updated between GPS fixes. Pathfinding always operated against the latest combined estimate.

Primary · Fare Logic

Fare Selection & Dispatch

Built the engine that picked the most profitable fare — not just the nearest one — by dividing payout by estimated completion time.

The system polled the competition server for available rides, then estimated total trip time including travel to pickup, the ride itself, and a turn-count penalty. Dividing fare by time gave a profit-per-minute score; the highest-scoring fare was claimed. This made the car actively optimise revenue rather than accepting rides sequentially.

Hardware · Physical Build

Brake & Turn Light Assembly

Hand-soldered the vehicle's brake and indicator light board onto a 15 × 8 cm protoboard, wired directly to Raspberry Pi GPIO.

Red and yellow LEDs were wired in series with 100 Ω current-limiting resistors, sized for the 3.3 V digital I/O output. The lights provided real-world signalling behaviour consistent with road traffic norms and were controlled programmatically alongside the navigation system.

Collaborative · Research

Computer Vision Direction

Contributed to technology selection for the CV stack — Coral TPU, EfficientDet-Lite, and Street View dataset strategy.

While implementation was led by a teammate, I had input on the choice of the Coral Edge TPU for hardware-accelerated inference, the EfficientDet-Lite model for its edge-device speed/accuracy balance, and the approach of sourcing training data from Google Street View imagery of Ontario roads.

Full System Breakdown

The remaining subsystems were built by teammates, with varying input from me on research and direction.

Lane Detection

Developed by a teammate, this system used OpenCV's Hough line transform to identify both solid white and dashed yellow lane markings from live camera frames. Each frame was pre-processed through grayscale conversion, Gaussian blur, binary thresholding, and Canny edge detection before a trapezoidal road mask was applied and lane lines were extracted.

A key insight: dotted yellow lines returned four Hough lines per dot (the edges of each rectangular segment), requiring a horizontal-line filter to discard stop-line noise and an outlier filter to consolidate valid lines into a single best-fit lane per side. All parameters were stored as global variables for rapid tuning. The algorithm performed consistently on straight paths; curved roads proved more difficult due to the Hough transform's linear assumptions.

Object Detection

Led by a teammate, this system ran a retrained EfficientDet-Lite model on a Google Coral Edge TPU — offloading inference from the Raspberry Pi's CPU entirely. The model was fine-tuned using a dataset of rubber ducks and road signs sourced from Google Street View imagery, annotated with bounding boxes via MakeSense.ai and exported in Pascal VOC format.

The deployed model produced real-time bounding box predictions with confidence scores. If confidence fell below 0.67 on a target class, the dataset was expanded and retraining repeated. In practice, the model performed well in static conditions but struggled with motion blur and variable lighting during active driving — a known gap that would have required a larger dataset and more moving-vehicle test sessions to resolve.

Motion Control

A proportional-derivative (PD) controller translated lane detection outputs into steering and speed commands. The proportional component (kp = 0.4) produced correction force proportional to lane-centre error; the derivative component (kd = 0.15) damped oscillation by tracking the rate of error change, preventing overshoot.

Speed was dynamically scaled across four zones: full speed for straight paths (<5° steering), 80% for gentle curves (5–10°), 60% for moderate curves (10–15°), and 40% for sharp curves (>15°). A recovery behaviour managed lane-loss: short gaps (≤10 frames) held the last known steering at 50% speed with 0.7× damping; prolonged loss (>10 frames) straightened the wheels and crept forward at 30%.

System Architecture

The software ran as a single central loop on the Raspberry Pi, calling modular functions on their respective polling schedules. Lane following ran every frame; VPFS position updates polled several times per second; fare checking on a longer interval.

Three clear layers: a perception layer (camera + grayscale sensors), a decision layer (pathfinding, fare selection, position tracking), and a control layer (PD steering, adaptive speed). Data flowed upward from sensors to decisions, commands downward to actuators. Each subsystem exposed simple interfaces to ease cross-team integration.

The main integration challenge was the simulation-to-real-course transition — physical track noise degraded lane detection, requiring parameter tightening. A steering servo mechanical imperfection caused rightward drift despite neutral commands, a hardware calibration issue rather than a software bug.

Outcome

All individual subsystems reached a functional baseline. The robot course-corrected via grayscale sensors, followed straight paths under camera guidance for ~7 seconds before drift, detected objects at adequate confidence in controlled conditions, and correctly executed pathfinding and fare selection logic.

Full integration exposed two main gaps — mechanical steering drift and reduced vision performance under motion — that would have needed further tuning to be competition-ready. The competition was cancelled before those issues were resolved.

Given the project scope, the team's limited prior experience with computer vision, embedded systems, and machine learning, and the compressed timeline alongside other coursework, the result reflects a meaningful technical achievement. The architecture was sound; remaining deficits were tuning problems, not design failures.
