Autonomous Driving

← Back to Design

Overview

A semester-long team project to build a fully autonomous robot capable of navigating a miniature city, accepting passenger fares, and obeying traffic rules — all in real time on the constrained hardware of a Raspberry Pi. The robot combined graph-based path planning, computer vision lane tracking, hardware-accelerated object detection, and a live fare-dispatch server to simulate a scaled-down autonomous taxi.

The competition was ultimately cancelled before deployment, but all major subsystems were built, integrated, and tested in controlled conditions. Individual components performed well in isolation; full-system autonomy was close but not complete within the available timeline.

My Contributions

What I Built

My work centred on the systems that determined where the vehicle went and why — the intelligence layer above raw motion. I also built the vehicle's brake and turn lights from scratch.

Primary · Navigation

Intelligent Pathfinding

Designed and implemented the route planning system using a modified Dijkstra's algorithm via NetworkX. Beyond shortest-path, edges were assigned custom priority weights — roads through a designated "risk zone" were penalized to make them appear longer, naturally avoiding them unless the profit gain was significant. Turn penalties further discouraged high-turn routes by doubling the effective distance of direction-changing branches, producing smoother and safer paths without hard-coded rules.

Primary · Fare Logic

Fare Selection & Dispatch

Built the fare-claiming engine that polled the competition server for available rides and selected them by profit-per-unit-time. The system estimated total trip duration — including travel to the pickup point, the ride itself, and a turn-count penalty — divided the fare by that estimate, and picked the highest-value option. This meant the car wasn't just accepting fares sequentially; it was actively optimising revenue per minute.

Primary · Position Tracking

Location Estimation

The competition's VPFS GPS system had a broadcast latency of up to 4 seconds — too slow for precise real-time navigation. I built a dead-reckoning fallback that estimated position by continuously polling vehicle speed and heading from the movement algorithms, keeping an internal x/y coordinate updated between GPS fixes. When the VPFS updated, the internal estimate was corrected. Pathfinding always operated against the latest combined estimate.

Hardware · Physical Build

Brake & Turn Light Assembly

Designed and hand-soldered the brake and indicator light board onto a 15 × 8 cm protoboard. Red and yellow LEDs were wired in series with 100 Ω current-limiting resistors, matched to the 3.3 V digital I/O output of the Raspberry Pi. Controlled directly from GPIO, the lights provided real-world signalling behaviour consistent with road traffic norms.

Collaborative · Research

Computer Vision Direction

Contributed to the research and technology selection for the computer vision stack — including the choice of the Coral Edge TPU for hardware-accelerated inference, the EfficientDet-Lite model architecture, and the dataset sourcing strategy using Google Street View imagery. While implementation was led by teammates, I had input on approach and methodology throughout.

Full System Breakdown

The remaining subsystems were developed by teammates. I had varying degrees of input and direction on each.

Lane Detection

Developed by a teammate, the lane detection system used OpenCV's Hough line transform to identify both solid white and dashed yellow lane markings from live camera frames. The pipeline pre-processed each frame through grayscale conversion, Gaussian blur, binary thresholding, and Canny edge detection before applying a trapezoidal road mask and extracting lane lines.

A key insight during development was that dotted yellow lines returned four lines per dot (the four edges of each rectangle), requiring a horizontal-line filter to discard stop-line noise and an outlier filter to consolidate valid lines into a single best-fit lane per side. All parameters were stored as global variables for rapid tuning.

Testing progressed from static simulation screenshots to real course footage, with each frame split into five diagnostic views during development. The algorithm performed consistently on straight paths, though curved roads proved more difficult due to Hough transform's linear assumptions.

Object Detection

Led by a teammate, the object detection system ran a retrained EfficientDet-Lite model on a Google Coral Edge TPU — offloading inference from the Raspberry Pi's CPU entirely. The model was fine-tuned in a Google Colab notebook using a dataset of rubber ducks and road signs sourced from Google Street View imagery of Ontario roads, annotated with bounding boxes via MakeSense.ai and exported in Pascal VOC format.

The deployed model produced real-time bounding box predictions with confidence scores. If confidence fell below 0.67 on a target object class, the dataset was expanded and retraining repeated. In practice, the model performed well in static conditions but struggled with motion blur and variable lighting during active driving.

I contributed to the research phase — specifically the decision to use the Coral TPU for acceleration and the EfficientDet-Lite architecture for its balance of speed and accuracy on edge hardware.

Motion Control

A proportional-derivative (PD) controller translated lane detection outputs into steering and speed commands. The proportional component (kp = 0.4) produced correction force proportional to lane-centre error; the derivative component (kd = 0.15) damped oscillation by tracking the rate of error change, preventing overshoot.

Steering handled three detection states: both lanes visible (target lane centre), right lane only (100 px inward offset), or left lane only (120 px rightward offset). Speed was dynamically scaled across four zones: full speed for straight paths (<5° steering), 80% for gentle curves (5–10°), 60% for moderate curves (10–15°), and 40% for sharp curves (>15°).

A recovery behaviour managed lane-loss: short gaps (≤10 frames) maintained the last known steering angle with 0.7× damping at 50% speed. Prolonged loss (>10 frames) straightened the wheels and crept forward at 30%, minimising uncontrolled drift.

System Architecture

The software ran as a single central loop on the Raspberry Pi, calling modular functions from each subsystem on their respective polling schedules. Lane following ran every frame; VPFS position updates were polled several times per second; fare checking happened on a longer interval.

The layered architecture separated concerns clearly: a perception layer (camera + grayscale sensors), a decision layer (pathfinding, fare selection, position tracking), and a control layer (PD steering, adaptive speed). Data flowed upward from sensors to decisions, and commands downward to actuators. Each subsystem exposed simple input/output interfaces to ease integration across the team.

The main integration challenge was moving from simulation to real course — noise and visual clutter on the physical track degraded lane detection performance, requiring parameter tightening. The steering servo also exhibited slight mechanical drift that caused rightward bias despite neutral steering commands, likely a calibration imperfection rather than a software issue.

Outcome

All individual subsystems reached a functional baseline within the semester. The robot could course-correct via grayscale sensors, follow straight paths under camera guidance for ~7 seconds before drift, detect objects with adequate confidence in controlled conditions, and execute pathfinding and fare selection logic correctly.

Full integration exposed gaps — most visibly a mechanical steering drift and reduced vision performance under motion — that would have required further tuning to be competition-ready. The competition was ultimately cancelled before those issues could be fully resolved.

Given the project scope, the team's limited prior experience with computer vision, embedded systems, and machine learning, and the compressed timeline alongside other coursework, the outcome reflects a meaningful technical achievement. The architecture was sound; the remaining deficits were tuning problems, not design failures.
